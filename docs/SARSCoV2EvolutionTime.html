Spike evolution of SARS-CoV-2

Viral infection elicits a humoral response and the production of antibodies with near-limitless diversity, which target residues on the surface of glycoproteins (spikes). Because of the high-density presentation of spikes on the viral surface, not all antigenic sites are targeted equally by antibodies. We used molecular dynamics simulation to estimate the ability of antibodies to target different surface residues of the SARS-CoV-2 spike.



For circulating viruses to propagate, they have to evade neutralization and recognition by antibodies and do so, by accumulating mutations at their spikes. We checked for the sarbecovirus subgenus spike (SARS family) if the mutational pattern matches the antibody pressure/affinity estimated by the model.

To compare the affinity map and the mutability map we applied spectral clustering. We aggregate residues to epitope clusters. The scatter plot shows how well the model does in explaining the mutability of the surface residue.
The correlation between the affinity map and the mutability map is 0.69 for the sarbecovirus subgenus spike. The simulation uses to estimate antibody pressure on a residue, only the surface geometry of the virus and the spike to compute the affinity map. Hence, the virus surface geometry gives rise to antibody pressure, as estimated by the model, that can explain 48% of the variability in the mutability map of the spike.
